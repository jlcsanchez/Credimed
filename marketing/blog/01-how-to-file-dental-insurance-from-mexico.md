# How to file a US dental insurance claim for work done in Mexico

**Meta description:** A step-by-step guide for US PPO members on filing out-of-network reimbursement claims for dental work performed in Mexico — what insurers actually need, where most claims fail, and how to avoid the wrong-CDT-code denial.

---

If you have a US PPO dental plan and you got dental work done in Mexico, **you can almost always get part of it reimbursed**. Most PPO plans pay out-of-network providers at somewhere between 50% and 80% of an "allowed amount" set by the insurer.

The problem isn't eligibility. The problem is the paperwork. Almost every plan accepts the claim — they just won't tell you which form, which codes, or which exact format will get it through their first-pass review without a denial.

This guide walks through the actual process, end to end, for filing one of these claims yourself. (Or, full disclosure: this is exactly what we built [Credimed](https://credimed.us) to handle for you, from $19 with a money-back guarantee. But if you'd rather DIY, here's the playbook.)

## The short version

1. Get an itemized receipt from your Mexican dentist (in Spanish is fine).
2. Translate each procedure to its US ADA CDT code.
3. Convert the peso amount to USD using the date-of-service exchange rate.
4. Fill out your insurer's out-of-network claim form (download from their member portal).
5. Attach the receipt + a copy of your insurance card front.
6. Mail or upload to your insurer's claims address.
7. Wait 14–45 days. Follow up if you hear nothing in 30.

## The longer version

### Step 1 — Get the right receipt

Mexican dentists usually issue two documents:

- A simple receipt (recibo) for the patient
- A formal CFDI / factura electrónica for tax purposes

For the insurance claim, **ask for the CFDI factura**. It includes the dentist's RFC (Mexico's tax ID), the licensed-provider details (cédula profesional), and an itemized list of every procedure with its date, description, and price.

If your dentist only gives you a thermal-paper receipt, ask them to email you a PDF of the factura. Every licensed dental clinic in Mexico is required to issue one — it's a tax-compliance requirement on their end, not a courtesy.

**Why this matters:** US insurers reject 80% of claims that don't include a verified provider tax ID. The CFDI is what proves the dentist is a real, licensed provider.

### Step 2 — Translate procedures to CDT codes

This is where most DIY claims die.

The American Dental Association maintains a standard code set called CDT (Current Dental Terminology). Every US insurer uses CDT codes to determine what they'll pay for. Your Mexican dentist used Mexican procedure descriptions — sometimes in Spanish, sometimes with regional variations.

You need to map each procedure on your factura to the right CDT code. The most common ones for Mexico dental tourism:

| Mexican description | CDT code | Description |
|---------------------|----------|-------------|
| Corona porcelana / cerámica | D2740 | Crown — porcelain/ceramic |
| Corona metal-cerámica | D2750 | Crown — porcelain fused to high noble metal |
| Tratamiento de conducto / endodoncia molar | D3330 | Endodontic therapy, molar |
| Endodoncia anterior | D3310 | Endodontic therapy, anterior |
| Limpieza profunda / curetaje | D4341 | Periodontal scaling and root planing, four or more teeth per quadrant |
| Limpieza simple / profilaxis | D1110 | Prophylaxis — adult |
| Implante dental | D6010 | Surgical placement of implant body |
| Pilar de implante / abutment | D6056 | Prefabricated abutment |
| Corona sobre implante | D6058 | Abutment-supported porcelain/ceramic crown |
| Extracción simple | D7140 | Extraction, erupted tooth |
| Extracción con cirugía | D7210 | Extraction, surgical removal |
| Empaste resina (1 superficie) | D2391 | Resin-based composite, one surface, posterior |
| Empaste resina (2 superficies) | D2392 | Resin-based composite, two surfaces, posterior |
| Blanqueamiento dental | D9972 | External bleaching (not covered by most PPOs) |
| Radiografía panorámica | D0330 | Panoramic radiographic image |

**Tip:** The exact CDT code matters a lot. A porcelain crown (D2740) and a porcelain-fused-to-metal crown (D2750) reimburse at different rates — one might be in your plan's allowed list and the other might not. If your factura just says "corona," ask your dentist to specify the material on a follow-up email. That email is admissible documentation.

### Step 3 — Convert pesos to USD

US insurers will not accept a claim in MXN. You have to give them a USD amount.

Use the **Bank of Mexico FIX rate** for the **date of service** (not the date you're filing). The Banco de México publishes a daily FIX rate at 12:00 PM CT — that's the recognized reference rate for cross-border conversions.

You can look it up here: [https://www.banxico.org.mx/SieInternet/](https://www.banxico.org.mx/SieInternet/) → Mercado Cambiario → Tipo de cambio FIX.

Round to the nearest cent. Document your conversion math on the claim form ("Converted at FIX rate of 17.42 MXN/USD on 2026-03-14 = $XXX.XX") so the adjuster doesn't have to redo your arithmetic.

### Step 4 — Get the right claim form

Every US PPO has its own out-of-network claim form. The standard form is **ADA Dental Claim Form (Form J430)** — most insurers accept it, and it's the format your dentist would use if they were in-network.

Download it from your insurer's member portal. Common direct links:

- **Aetna:** Member portal → Claims → "Submit a claim"
- **Cigna:** myCigna → Claims & Payments → "Submit a claim"
- **Delta Dental:** memberportal.deltadental.com → Claims tab
- **MetLife:** mybenefits.metlife.com → Dental → Submit claim
- **Guardian:** guardiananytime.com → Manage claims

Fill in:

- Member ID + group number (front of your insurance card)
- Date of service
- Provider name + address (your Mexican dentist)
- Provider tax ID (the RFC from the CFDI)
- Each CDT code with its USD amount
- Total amount paid

### Step 5 — Attach supporting documents

Always include:

- The CFDI factura (PDF)
- Front of your insurance card (photo or scan)
- Translated explanation of each procedure (a one-page cover letter is fine)

Sometimes include:

- Pre-treatment x-rays (if you have them — strengthens claims for crowns and root canals)
- Treatment plan from the dentist (especially for multi-visit work)
- Proof of payment (credit card statement showing the charge)

### Step 6 — Submit

Most insurers accept claims by:

- **Online upload** (fastest — usually 14–21 days to first decision)
- **Email** (sometimes faster, sometimes not)
- **Postal mail** (slowest — add 7–10 days)

If you're mailing, send certified with return receipt so you have proof of delivery.

### Step 7 — Track and resubmit if needed

Most claims process in 14–45 days. If you hear nothing in 30, call the member services number on your insurance card and reference your claim number.

If you get a denial, **read the denial code carefully**. The most common ones:

- **CO-50** — non-covered service. Usually wrong CDT code; resubmit with the correct one.
- **CO-29** — time limit for filing has expired. You missed the window (most plans give you 12 months from date of service).
- **CO-204** — service not covered by the patient's current benefit plan. This may mean the procedure truly isn't covered, OR the adjuster looked at the wrong section. Worth one resubmission.
- **CO-16** — claim/service lacks information needed for adjudication. Almost always fixable. They'll tell you what's missing in the explanation of benefits (EOB).

Resubmit within 30 days of the denial with the corrected info and a note explaining what changed.
